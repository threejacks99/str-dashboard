---
title: "Understanding Net Operating Income (NOI)"
category: "Understanding Your Metrics"
---

Net Operating Income is the headline profit number on your **Financials** page. It answers the question every owner actually cares about: after the money comes in and the bills go out, what's left?

## How Hostics calculates it

```
NOI = Total Revenue − Total Expenses
```

Both pieces are specific to how Hostics defines them:

- **Total Revenue** is the sum of your **owner payouts** across all bookings in the selected date range — what you actually received, not the gross nightly rate. Cancelled bookings and personal/owner stays are excluded.
- **Total Expenses** is the sum of every expense whose paid date falls in the range, across all categories.

Revenue is bucketed by **check-in date** and expenses by **paid date**, so both reflect activity within the period you've selected at the top of the page.

## What NOI does and doesn't include

NOI is an *operating* profit figure. It reflects the income and expenses you record in Hostics — including any mortgage interest, property tax, or management fees you enter as expenses. It is **not** a tax calculation: it doesn't apply depreciation, and it won't match your taxable income line-for-line. For tax, use the **Schedule E export** on the Reports page, which formats your data the way the IRS form expects.

Because NOI is only as complete as the expenses you enter, an unusually high NOI often just means some costs haven't been imported yet. If a month looks too good, check that its expenses are in. See [Setting up your expense tracking](/help/importing-expenses).

## The Operating Expense Ratio (OER)

Alongside NOI, your Financials page shows OER:

```
OER = Total Expenses ÷ Total Revenue × 100
```

This is the share of your income consumed by expenses. A lower percentage means more of every dollar reaches your pocket. It's most useful watched over time — a steadily climbing OER is an early signal that costs are outpacing income.

## The NOI trend

The NOI trend chart plots monthly NOI (that month's owner-payout revenue minus that month's expenses) so you can see seasonality and direction at a glance. Use the date-range and property filters to zoom into a season or a single rental.

## Reading it well

- **Compare periods.** Use the period-over-period comparison to see whether NOI is up or down versus the prior equivalent stretch — a single number means little without a baseline.
- **Filter by property.** For a portfolio, NOI across all properties hides which ones are actually carrying the business. Filter to each property to find your winners and your drains.
- **Keep expenses current.** NOI is your most important number and your easiest to accidentally overstate. Import expenses regularly.
